The Importance of Synthetic Biology

Synthetic biology represents a transformative leap in biotechnology, enabling breakthroughs in:

1953: DNA Discovery

1953: Discovery of DNA

James Watson and Francis Crick discovered the double-helix structure of DNA, laying the foundation for genetic engineering and future biological research.

1973: Plasmids Used in Genetic Engineering

1973: Plasmids in Genetic Engineering

Scientists Herbert Boyer and Stanley Cohen developed genetic engineering techniques using plasmids as vectors, ushering in the era of DNA recombination.

1982: First Synthetic Insulin

1982: First Synthetic Insulin

Using recombinant DNA technology, synthetic human insulin was produced, revolutionizing diabetes treatment and marking a breakthrough in synthetic biology in medicine.

2004: Foundation of iGEM

2004: iGEM Competition Established

The International Genetically Engineered Machine (iGEM) competition was founded, encouraging students worldwide to create innovative synthetic biology projects, advancing education and research in the field.

2012: CRISPR-Cas9 Gene Editing

2012: CRISPR-Cas9 Gene Editing

The CRISPR-Cas9 technology enabled simpler and more efficient gene editing, transforming research methods in agriculture and medicine.

2017: Synthetic Chromosomes Created

2017: Creation of Synthetic Chromosomes

Scientists successfully synthesized yeast chromosomes, marking a significant step forward in designing new organisms at the cellular level.

2024: Over 75,000 BioParts

2024: Over 75,000 BioBricks Parts

By 2024, the global synthetic biology repository accumulated over 75,000 standardized genetic components, accelerating research and development in synthetic biology.

Complex Tools

Time-Consuming Design Cycles

Designing synthetic components is slow and laborious, involving extensive trial and error.

Fragmented Databases

Synthetic biology databases lack real-time updates, making it hard to access the latest findings cohesively.

Limited Collaboration

The lack of integrated solutions results in inefficient workflows, slowing down innovation.

Low Public Awareness

Synthetic biology remains relatively unknown to the public, hindering its growth and societal acceptance.
